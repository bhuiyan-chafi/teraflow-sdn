\begin{abstract}
Flexible-grid optical networks improve spectrum efficiency but introduce additional challenges for Routing and Spectrum Allocation (RSA). These constraints become even more critical in the presence of parallel links between network nodes, where multiple links interconnect the same pair of nodes, causing a combinatorial growth of feasible routing options and making conventional SDN-based path computation inefficient. This is further complicated by strict spectrum continuity and contiguity constraints requiring fine-grained slot management.

This paper presents \emph{ParallelOpticalController}, a microservice integrated into \emph{TeraFlow} SDN ecosystem to address both routing scalability and spectrum allocation complexity. The solution models the network as a multi-directed graph to efficiently compute shortest and alternative paths, and employs a bitwise algorithm to rapidly identify contiguous spectrum slots across end-to-end paths.

With respect to solutions adopted in other SDN controllers, the proposed approach significantly reduces computational overhead while improving resource utilization, enabling scalable control of optical networks with parallel-link topologies. Furthermore, the work investigates key design parameters, such as the number of candidate paths and spectrum assignment policies, to identify the trade-offs that provide the best balance between scalability and resource utilization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} %Max 1 page
%1. Introduce the general context (flex-grid optical networks and RSA).
The paradigm shift in global data consumption, driven by bandwidth-intensive applications such as real-time ultra-high-definition streaming, large-scale High-Performance Computing, and the pervasive deployment of AI agents, has placed unprecedented pressure on optical core infrastructures~\cite{decix2025traffic}. To accommodate this growth, transport networks transitioned from traditional fixed-grid Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) toward more versatile Elastic Optical Networks (EONs) based on flexible grid architectures. Under the ITU-T G.694.1 standard\cite{ituG6941}, the optical spectrum is discretized into small Frequency Slot Units (FSUs). The nominal central frequency is defined by the formula: $f_c = 193.1 THz + (n \times 0.00625) THz$, where $n$ is an integer number. The width of the allocated slot ($\Delta f$) is determined by: $\Delta f = 12.5 GHz \times m$, where $m$ is a positive integer representing the number of contiguous slots. This granularity enables diverse bit rates to coexist within the same fiber, maximizing the total throughput. While EONs offer significant flexibility, they introduce the Routing and Spectrum Allocation (RSA) problem, a complex computational challenge in optical network management.

%2. Briefly mention relevant literature (only key references).
The complexity grows with respect to the number of nodes and edges in a topology, and the fundamental complexity was formally established as NP-hard~\cite{wangRSAInfocom}. Wu et al.~\cite{wuDynamicRSAEON} address this through a joint RSA framework incorporating multipath selection, spectrum partitioning, resource sharing, and next-state-aware (NSA) assignment. Their approach utilizes offline MILP-calculated probabilities for online path selection and a partitioning scheme with an NSA algorithm designed to minimize network-wide capacity loss. Similarly, Srivastava et al.~\cite{srivaSpectrumStatusRSA} propose Spectrum Status Aware RSA (SS-RSA), which employs a modified Dijkstra’s algorithm with dynamic link weights derived from available contiguous spectrum slices. Final spectrum assignment is performed using First-Fit (FF) or First-Last Fit policies under standard continuity and contiguity constraints to reduce blocking probability. Alam et al. in~\cite{alamMHLS} further extended these resource-aware heuristics by proposing the Minimum Hops with Least Slots (MHLS) algorithm for sliced EON systems using sliceable bandwidth variable transponders (SBVT). Their methodology involves calculating the number of hops ($H$) and the required FSUs for each request to derive a novel ordering parameter, $NP=FSUs\times H$. By sorting traffic demands in ascending order of $NP$ and serving them sequentially, the algorithm optimizes traffic grooming and minimizes spectral occupancy compared to traditional benchmark policies.
%3. Mention the problem (impact of parallel links on scalability).
However, the scalability of these algorithmic solutions is often challenged when multiple fibers (parallel links) are routed through the same Wavelength Selective Switch (WSS). In this case frequency reuse is inhibited, causing the computational search space to expand exponentially.

%4. Highlight limitations of existing solutions (especially controller implementation)
Traditional SDON controllers treat parallel links as discrete entities, executing recursive RSA sequentially across individual paths until a valid allocation is identified. In high-density environments, this exhaustive search triggers exponential time complexity, creating computational bottlenecks that severely limit control plane scalability and resource efficiency.
%5. Summarize the main contributions of the paper (including contributions to Teraflow).
To address these bottlenecks, this paper introduces an optimized ParallelOpticalController (POC) integrated into the TeraFlowSDN (TFS) ecosystem implementing shortest path algorithm and parallel link aware spectrum assignment. 
\section{Background and Problem Statement} %Half a page, include some references
%1. Define the RSA problem in flex-grid networks.
The RSA problem in flex-grid (EONs) is an evolution of the Routing and Wavelength Assignment (RWA) problem found in traditional fixed-grid WDM systems \cite{chatterjeeRSATutorial}. The fundamental difference lies in the EON's ability to adaptively partition the optical spectrum into variable-sized slots to match specific bitrate demands. Common approaches to RSA involve utilizing the K-Shortest Path algorithm for route discovery and the FF heuristic for spectrum assignment \cite{yangHoldingAwareRSA}. Additionally, guard bands must be implemented between adjacent channels to mitigate inter-channel interference and ensure a high quality of transmission \cite{chrisEBAFlex, patelRSAWDM}. RSA problems are categorized into two scenarios, static where traffic demands are known in advance, and dynamic, where connection requests arrive stochastically\cite{zhangOptimalRSA, velascoSolvingRSA}. %In dynamic environments, the pursuit of high Quality of Service (QoS) necessitates efficient heuristics for rapid resource allocation, the feasibility of which is strictly governed by two fundamental spectral constraints.
%2. Explain key constraints (spectrum continuity and contiguity).
The RSA algorithm must find a path and then allocate a specific number of frequency slots to satisfy the bandwidth requirement. This allocation is strictly governed by the \textit{spectrum continuity constraint}, which mandates that the same indices are utilized across every fiber link along the selected path. Simultaneously, the \textit{spectrum contiguity constraint} requires that these slots be adjacent to one another in the frequency spectrum \cite{recalcatiBenefitsOfEON}. If a network controller fails to identify a block of frequency slots that satisfies both conditions, the lightpath request is blocked, regardless of the total aggregate free capacity. These fundamental constraints become significantly more complex with parallel links.
%3. Describe the effect of parallel links on routing complexity.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{Icton/graphics/issues_with_parallel_links.png}
    \caption{Effect of Parallel Links on RSA}
    \label{fig:rsa_issue_parallel_links}
\end{figure}
 As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:rsa_issue_parallel_links}, a lightpath request from $src$ and $Dst$ traverses nodes A, B, and C, which are interconnected via three physically independent parallel fibers. For topologies with uniform number of parallel links between nodes, the complexity scales as $P^H$ for a path of length $H$ (hops) with $P$ links per hop, while in non-uniform cases it is generalized as $\prod_{k=1}^H P_k$, where $P_k$ represents the links available at each hop $k$. Effective RSA must simultaneously resolve the spatial selection of paths and the allocation of contiguous spectral slots to avoid blocking. 
\section{The Parallel Optical Controller} %Max 1.5 page including the subsections
%1. Brief intro on the TeraFlow ecosystem
The \textit{POC} is an independent microservice within the \textit{TFS} ecosystem which is a state-of-the-art, open-source SDN controller developed under the European Telecommunications Standards Institute to radically advance the orchestration of beyond 5G and 6G networks\cite{tfsTD, tfsIPvirtual, tfsAI}. Designed as a secure, cloud-native platform, \textit{TFS} employs a microservices-based architecture orchestrated within Kubernetes environments, enabling individual control components to operate autonomously and scale independently. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:tfs_architecture} the architecture is explicitly engineered to seamlessly integrate with modern Network Function Virtualization (NFV) and Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) frameworks, load-balancing and security protection.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:poc_service_orch_3}, the initiation of a lightpath request via the \textit{TFS} service component generates a service request object exposed to the \textit{POC}. The \textit{POC} constructs the network topology by querying relevant data from the \textit{TFS} context and device services . This topology is transmitted to the \textit{RSA Engine}, which utilizes specialized functions in \textit{RSA Tools} for graph generation and path computation. Upon identifying a suitable route, \textit{RSA Tools} executes spectrum assignment—encompassing bitmap pre-processing, alignment, and slot allocation—consistent with ITU-T G.694.1 standards as utilized in the simulation environment . Finally, the resulting lightpath configuration is provisioned and presented through the TeraFlow web service component.

%1. Accurate description of the implemented solution
% --- Routing over Multi-Directed Graphs
To achieve computationally efficient routing, the algorithm utilizes the \textit{NetworkX}\cite{networkX} library for complex network analysis. The RSA Engine converts the initial multi-graph instance \textit{G} into a simplified graph abstraction, $G_{simple}$, in which parallel physical links between nodes are collapsed into a single logical edge. By routing over $G_{simple}$ rather than a raw physical multi-graph, the controller prevents the state-space explosion and latency. The algorithm first identifies the shortest paths via Dijkstra's algorithm and additional candidate paths satisfying the hop-count constraint $H\leq H_{min}+\Delta H$, where $H_{min}$ is the shortest path length and $\Delta H$ is the allowed additional hops. These paths are subsequently projected back onto the initial multi-directed graph \textit{G} for parallel path expansion.
% --- Bitwise Spectrum Allocation
Following the path selection, the controller initiates the spectrum allocation phase based on $N_r$, as defined in Table~\ref{tab:fsu_definitions}, establishing a \textit{reference\_bitmap} from the maximum FSU capacity, $N_h$, found among all $N_s$ values. Each endpoint's local bitmap $N_s$ is then aligned with this reference to ensure bitwise spectral continuity across links with heterogeneous frequency ranges. The algorithm is presented as follows:

\vspace{0.1cm}
\noindent\textbf{Algorithm 1} RSA with parallel links
\vspace{0.1cm}
\hrule
\vspace{0.2cm}

\begin{algorithmic}[1] % [1] adds line numbers
    \REQUIRE Graph $G=(V, E)$, Usable Links $\mathcal{L}$, Lightpath Request $\mathcal{R}$, Total Path Length $\mathcal{H}$ in hops
    \STATE \textbf{Step 1:} Create graph $G$
    \FORALL{$link \in \mathcal{L}$}
    \STATE add devices as $V$, links as $E$ in $G$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE \textbf{Step 2:} Path computation
    \STATE convert $G$ into $G_{simple}$
    \STATE $dijkstra_{path}$=find\_path($G_{simple}$, RF)
    \STATE $additional_{path}$=find\_path($G_{simple}$, $H$, RF)
    \STATE \textbf{Step 2.1:} Select desired path, $path\_node$
    \STATE \textbf{Step 3:} Perform Spectrum Assignment
    \STATE calculate $B\_r$, $N\_r$
    \FORALL{$hop \in path\_node$}
    \STATE get link information
    \STATE find $min, max$ frequencies
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE calculate $reference\_bitmap$
    \FORALL{$hop \in path\_node$}
    \STATE get source, destination endpoint bitmaps
    \STATE align them with $reference\_bitmap$
    \STATE perform intersection \& update $reference\_bitmap$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE \textbf{Step 3.1:} apply FSU selection scheme on $bitamp\_final$
    \IF{success}
    \STATE $\mathcal{R}$ is \textbf{success}
    \ELSE
    \STATE $\mathcal{R}$ is \textbf{blocked}
    \ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}

\section{Performance Evaluation} %One page including at least 2 results figures
To assess the performance of the \textit{POC}, we performed two distinct evaluations to determine both efficiency and statistical network performance. The efficiency evaluation is conducted within the TFS ecosystem by issuing a lightpath request to the controller and recording the execution time across four sequential phases: Graph Generation (GG), Shortest Path Computation (SPC), RSA Computation (RSAC), and Additional Path Computation (APC). Finally, the efficiency ($\mathcal{E}$) is measured by comparing the execution time using the formula: $\mathcal{E}=T_{single} / T_{parallel}$. The tested topologies are—Linear (LS, LP), and NSFNet (NS, NP)—with each evaluated in both a standard single-link baseline and a highly dense parallel configuration. All execution metrics are measured in seconds.

The proposed controller was evaluated using the NSFNET topology but in two different configurations. The first configuration is considered as the benchmark with 14 Nodes, 22 links and the parallel one with 14 Nodes, 66 links (3x parallel links). Experiments were conducted across varying traffic loads at a dynamic bitrate (50\% of 100Gbps, 25\% of 200Gbps and 25\% of 400Gbps). The spectral environment adheres to the \textit{ITU-T} C-Band standard, featuring 701 frequency slots with a fine-grained 6.25 GHz granularity.
\subsection{Simulation setup and metrics}
To perform the dynamic traffic simulation, a simulator has been designed using Python's Flask microframework outside the TFS ecosystem. Later, it is containerized using Docker and orchestrated via Docker Compose. Lightpath provisioning requests are dynamically generated following a Poisson process and uniformly distributed among the source-destination pairs. The inter-arrival time and holding time of the lightpath requests are exponentially distributed with an average of $1/\lambda$ and $1/\mu$ seconds, respectively. The load offered to the network in provisioning state condition is expressed in Erlangs as the ratio $\lambda/\mu$.
\subsection{Simulation results}
The test cases are designed to evaluate the comparative performance of several path selection schemes: first-fit (FF), random-fit (RF), and spectrum-check (SC). Both the SC and RF strategies are further assessed under a configuration allowing for an additional hop beyond the shortest path count (SP+1). Given that spectrum assignment first-fit (SAFF) is widely adopted as the industry standard, a comparative configuration using random-fit spectrum assignment (SPRF-SARF) is included to establish a performance baseline. The simulation results demonstrate that the (SC) path selection scheme consistently achieves superior performance. This outcome is expected, as the scheme explicitly evaluates the congestion level of each candidate path to select the least congested route. Analysis across varying network loads reveals that SPSC maintains a blocking probability below\footnote{The result is considered at a 72\% condifidence interval. Even though the result is considered unstable, it is evaluated with 7M lightpath requests to reach the conclusion.} $10^{-4}$, whereas SPFF and SPRF typically range between $10^{-3}$ and $10^{-4}$. Notably, (SP+1SC-SAFF) is outperformed by the shortest-path schemes; this indicates that the inclusion of additional links increases the total resource occupancy, thereby raising the probability of spectral contention across the network. The results remain consistent in the topology featuring three parallel links under network loads scaled by a factor of three. The shortest-path spectrum-check heuristic, integrated with parallel-path random-fit and first-fit spectrum assignment (SPSCPRF-SAFF), achieves the most efficient results by intelligently minimizing spectral contention. Shortest-path first-fit and random-fit schemes utilizing parallel-path random-fit (SPFFPRF and SPRFPRF) exhibit behavior similar to the trends observed in the single-link configuration. Finally, the random-fit spectrum assignment scheme performs poorly even when paired with the superior path selection strategy (SPSCPRF-SARF), confirming that inefficient spectral allocation significantly increases fragmentation and leads to higher blocking probabilities in parallel architectures.

\section{Conclusion} %5-8 lines
% 1. Be short here, use past tense briefly recall what you did and which is the main results
This paper we developed a network controller and an optimized RSA framework that utilized bitwise algorithms and simplified graph abstractions to manage the computational complexity inherent in parallel-link topologies. The solution was validated through both functional execution time measurements and dynamic traffic simulations across multiple reference topologies. Experimental results demonstrated that the proposed mechanism achieved high computational efficiency and maintained linear scalability as network density increased. Furthermore, statistical analysis revealed that while the PARS-FF scheme was effective under low loads, the PARS-RF approach significantly reduced blocking probabilities in high-load and high-bitrate scenarios by balancing traffic across parallel resources.
