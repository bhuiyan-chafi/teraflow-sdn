## Chapter 1

### Internet in Modern Era

In 2025, approximately 6 billion people use the internet, and this accounts for nearly 74% of the global population. While mobile broadband traffic is growing rapidly, fixed networks still carry the vast majority of global data. This immense data flow is driven by modern digital applications, which demand continuous and high-bandwidth data transmission. High-definition video conferencing requires highly stable connectivity, whereas cloud-based gaming strictly demands low-latency data transfer. Additionally, the widespread deployment of connected sensors generates heavy uplink traffic, because live security surveillance operates continuously around the clock.

Furthermore, recent advancements in artificial intelligence have altered network demands, and these technologies introduce highly unpredictable traffic patterns. The distributed training of large machine learning models requires massive data synchronization, which frequently causes sudden traffic bursts across data centers. As machine-to-machine communications expand globally, overall data volumes are projected to maintain a steep upward trajectory. Satisfying this escalating demand requires robust infrastructure, but accommodating such immense scale was not always possible in the past. The capacity to sustain today's interconnected world relies on fundamental physical transformations, which defined the historical evolution of communication networks.

### Evolution of the Network

The evolution of telecommunication began with simple point-to-point analog systems. Early infrastructure relied on copper cables and circuit switching to establish dedicated physical paths for telephone calls. As the need for digital computer communication emerged, circuit switching proved highly inefficient for sporadic data traffic. This limitation drove the transition to packet switching in the late 1960s. Researchers developed ARPANET, which broke data into discrete packets that could dynamically share the same physical transmission lines. At the physical layer, transmission mediums slowly evolved to handle higher frequencies. Networks upgraded from basic twisted-pair copper to coaxial cables, and eventually to optical fibers. This physical evolution was necessary to overcome severe bandwidth limitations and signal attenuation over long distances.

As these early isolated networks expanded, a critical problem emerged regarding interoperability. Different networks used proprietary rules and could not communicate with one another. To resolve this fragmentation, researchers developed the Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol (TCP/IP) suite. This universal standard provided a common language, allowing diverse systems to reliably route data across multiple network boundaries. Concurrently, growing traffic volumes overwhelmed early network hubs. To prevent network congestion, engineers developed intelligent devices like routers and switches to direct packets efficiently. Furthermore, signal degradation in long-haul links was solved by introducing advanced physical layer devices. The deployment of optical amplifiers and specialized optical transponders allowed data to travel across continents without requiring continuous electrical regeneration.

Network evolution navigated four distinct phases to reach the modern internet infrastructure. The first phase consisted of legacy analog telecommunications focused entirely on voice traffic. The second phase introduced digital packet switching and the foundational TCP/IP protocols during the research-driven ARPANET era. The third phase brought commercialization and public accessibility, marked by the World Wide Web and the deployment of consumer broadband. Finally, the current fourth phase features a globally integrated infrastructure. This modern phase relies on a dense optical fiber backbone, pervasive wireless access networks, and dynamic routing protocols to support billions of concurrent users and massive data loads. As traffic exploded during this current phase, laying new physical cables to meet capacity demands became economically and practically impossible. To solve this bottleneck, engineers applied a concept similar to dividing a single-lane road into a multi-lane highway. Instead of transmitting one data stream over a single beam of light, they transmitted multiple distinct colors, or wavelengths, of light simultaneously through the exact same fiber. Each wavelength operates as an independent, high-speed data channel. This breakthrough technology is known as Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM), and it serves as the essential high-capacity foundation for today's backbone networks.

### WDM and Elastic Optical Network

Early optical networks transmitted a single data stream using one wavelength of light per fiber. Industry standards like Synchronous Optical Networking (SONET) and Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH) managed these single-channel systems. However, as global internet traffic surged, installing new physical fiber cables to meet capacity demands became economically impractical. To maximize the capacity of existing infrastructure, the telecommunications industry introduced Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM). WDM allows multiple optical signals to be transmitted simultaneously over a single fiber by assigning each signal a distinctly different wavelength of laser light.

The WDM standard evolved into two primary variations to serve different network requirements. Coarse Wavelength Division Multiplexing (CWDM) uses wide spacing between wavelengths. It is generally deployed for shorter distances, such as metropolitan area networks, where cost-effective equipment is prioritized over maximum capacity. Conversely, Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing (DWDM) packs wavelengths much closer together. DWDM supports dozens or even hundreds of independent channels on a single fiber, making it the standard for high-capacity, long-haul backbone networks.

To handle the management burden of these high-capacity, multi-fiber infrastructures, the optical control plane required continuous evolution. Early network provisioning relied on static, manual configurations. Over time, the industry transitioned to distributed control plane standards, such as Generalized Multiprotocol Label Switching (GMPLS). Today, modern backbone networks utilize highly automated, centralized management systems empowered by Software-Defined Networking (SDN).

As DWDM technologies matured, the traditional method of allocating optical spectrum became a structural bottleneck. Early DWDM systems relied on a fixed-grid architecture. This fixed grid assigned rigid, uniform channel spacing—such as 50 GHz intervals—regardless of the actual bandwidth required by the data signal. This rigidity resulted in wasted optical spectrum when smaller signals were transmitted. To overcome this limitation, flexible-grid (flex-grid) architectures emerged to create Elastic Optical Networks (EON). As seen in Figure XX, flex-grid networks adhere to ITU-T G.694.1 standards, which define a precise 6.25 GHz slot granularity and a 12.5 GHz minimum slot width. This flexible architecture allows for elastic, demand-driven bandwidth allocation. By assigning only the exact spectrum required for an individual connection, flex-grid vastly improves the overall spectral efficiency of the network.

### Network Planes

Modern networks are conceptually divided into distinct layers of operation. The two primary layers are the data plane and the control plane. The data plane is responsible for the actual forwarding of user traffic. In an optical network, the data plane consists of physical hardware. Optical transceivers convert incoming electrical data into optical signals and then these signals travel through the Reconfigurable Optical Add-Drop Multiplexers (ROADMs). ROAMs switch and direct specific wavelengths of light toward their correct destinations without converting them back to electrical signals. The control plane acts as the brain of the network. It discovers the network topology, calculates routes, and sends configuration commands to the physical devices. Historically, the optical control plane relied on distributed systems like Generalized Multiprotocol Label Switching (GMPLS). In a distributed architecture, there is no central brain. Instead, the routing decision is shared among the nodes. Every single router or ROADM independently runs routing algorithms and constantly exchanges network state information with its neighbors.

As optical networks expanded, this distributed approach became highly complex to manage. To solve this, the industry shifted toward a centralized management system. This approach is known as Software-Defined Networking (SDN). SDN physically separates the control plane from the data plane. It removes the routing decision process from the individual hardware devices and places it into a centralized software controller.
